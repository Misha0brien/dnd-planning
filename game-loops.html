Theoretical Foundation

Game loop theory, originating in video game design, describes the repeating cycles of action and reward that sustain player engagement over time. A well-constructed game presents players with layered loops operating at different timescales: immediate moment-to-moment decisions nested inside session-level goals, which are in turn nested inside campaign-spanning stakes.

Loop Central Design Question Scope
Meta Why do players invest in the outcome? Entire campaign
Progress How do players perceive advancement between sessions? Multi-session arcs
Core What repeated activities structure individual sessions? Session / scene
⚔ Design Order

Design proceeds from the outside in: Meta Loop first, then Progress Loops, then Core Loops. Reversing this order produces campaigns where activity is abundant but stakes are unclear — or where stakes are defined but unsupported by session content.

Step 01 Establish Setting Tone & Inspirational Sources ▶

Before any loop design begins, establish the campaign's aesthetic and emotional register. This functions as a constraint set — all subsequent design decisions should be legible within it.

Procedure

  1. Compile 5–10 reference works — fiction, film, games, mythology — that collectively define the intended tone. They don't need to share a genre; they should share a feel.
  2. Write a 2–3 sentence campaign premise that names the central conflict and the players' relationship to it.
  3. Identify the dominant emotional mode: triumph, tragedy, dread, wonder, moral ambiguity, etc.

Diagnostic Questions

  • What does daily life feel like in this world for an ordinary person?
  • What distinguishes this setting from a generic high-fantasy baseline?
  • What types of scenes and conflicts will recur? Do the reference works support this?
✦ Example — Mythic Odyssey Campaign

Reference works: Greek & World Mythology, Hercules, The Odyssey, Sinbad, Percy Jackson, Monster Hunter, Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind.

Premise: Mythic heroes, marked by prophecy, must navigate a world in which the gods are active participants and the Titans threaten to unmake civilization. Glory is earned; fate is contested.

Emotional register: Epic — heroic sacrifice, divine favor, and the weight of destiny.

Step 02 Construct the Meta Loop Campaign Scale ▶

The Meta Loop is the campaign's primary structure of meaning. It answers the question every player implicitly asks across every session: why does this matter, and what is at stake if we fail?

A complete Meta Loop has three required components.

2a — The Inciting Hook

The hook is the narrative and mechanical reason these particular characters are involved in this particular conflict. It must establish personal stakes — the players must stand to lose something specific, not merely witness generic peril.

Key Distinction

The hook is not the premise. The premise explains what the world is; the hook explains why the players' characters cannot ignore it.

Structural Requirements

  • Addresses why these characters are implicated, rather than any available adventurers
  • Establishes what the characters stand to lose through inaction
  • Creates an initial relationship between the party and the central conflict

2b — The Antagonist's Escalation Chain

The antagonist must be actively pursuing a goal, representable as a linear chain of escalating steps — each of which is a potential intervention point for the players.

Each node represents a stage of the threat. Player action can interrupt, accelerate, or redirect the chain. The chain also functions as a pacing mechanism: the campaign advances when the antagonist advances.

2c — Branching Terminal States

Define at minimum two distinct possible endings. Terminal states should be concrete and world-level — not abstract moral terms.

✗ Failure State

What the world becomes if the antagonist succeeds. Described from the perspective of ordinary people in the world.

✓ Success State

What the players can achieve by stopping them. Specific, world-level consequences — achievable through multiple approaches if possible.

✦ Example — Mythic Odyssey Meta Loop

Hook: A prophecy designates the player characters as the Chosen Ones whose actions will determine Thylea's fate. Inaction is not neutral — the prophecy will be fulfilled with or without their participation.

Escalation Chain: Prophecy activates → God seeks ritual method → God acquires components → God executes ritual, freeing the Titans.

Terminal States: Thylea Destroyed (failure) / Thylea Saved (success).

Step 03 Design Progress Loops Arc Scale ▶

Progress Loops are the mid-level systems through which players perceive meaningful advancement between sessions. Without them, sessions exist in narrative isolation — events occur but accumulate no structural significance.

Design Principles

MULTIPLICITY

Sustain 2–4 simultaneous loops. Different players orient toward different tracks.

VISIBILITY

Players must observe their standing. An invisible loop produces no motivational effect.

DIFFERENTIATION

Loops should reward distinct modes of play — not all combat, not all social.

Step 04 Design Core Loops Session Scale ▶

Core Loops are the repeating activity structures that give sessions their shape — the specific things players do within any given session. Design a portfolio of 5–7 distinct types, rotating through them across sessions.

The Repetition–Variety Axis

Each individual loop may repeat frequently enough to develop player mastery. The campaign retains variety through rotation across the portfolio — not by endlessly novelizing any single loop.

Step 05 Wire the Loops Together ▶

Once all three loop types are designed, they must be connected into a coherent system. Actions in Core Loops produce consequences that advance Progress Loops; accumulated Progress Loop advancement brings the Meta Loop's terminal states within reach.

The Wiring Test

For each Core Loop, verify: (1) Which Progress Loop(s) does successful completion advance? (2) How does engaging with this loop make the Meta Loop's stakes feel tangible? A Core Loop that fails both tests is an isolated activity — entertaining in isolation, but contributing nothing to campaign momentum.

Step 06 Design Entry Hooks for Each Core Loop ▶

A Core Loop that players cannot find cannot function. For each Core Loop, prepare a minimum of three entry hooks — specific, concrete fictional circumstances that naturally draw the party into that loop's activity structure.

Hook Taxonomy

Hook TypeDefinitionDesign Consideration
Personal Tied to a specific PC's backstory, relationships, or home Guarantees at least one player is invested before the loop begins
Geographic Located along the party's current or planned travel route Low-friction; loop is encountered rather than sought
Reactive Triggered by something the party has already done Rewards player agency; loops feel like consequences
Proactive Pushed toward the party by antagonist action or world events Maintains narrative pressure; ensures no loop atrophies from neglect
Minimum Hook Portfolio

For any given Core Loop: at minimum one Personal hook + one Geographic hook + one Reactive or Proactive hook.

Appendix — One-Shots & Short Adventures

For single-session or short-arc (2–4 session) scenarios, the three-loop hierarchy collapses to two levels. The wiring principle remains unchanged.

StandardShort-Form Equivalent
Meta LoopReplaced by the session's primary objective; stakes are immediate rather than campaign-spanning
Progress Loop1–2 loops; advancement visible within the session rather than across sessions
Core Loop1–2 loops; sufficient variety to sustain a single session
✦ Example — One-Shot Heist

Session objective (Meta substitute): Steal the artifact before dawn.

Progress Loop: Plan → Execute → Escape. Players track mission phase as visible progress.

Core Loops (2): Infiltration (evade guards / gather intelligence / locate the vault) and The Heist (breach / manage complications / extract safely). Each feeds the Progress Loop stage.
